1. Introduction to Fairness in CPU Scheduling: Concepts and Challenges
Fairness in CPU scheduling is a fundamental yet complex dimension of operating system
design. While traditional scheduling evaluation has focused on performance metrics like
throughput and turnaround time, fairness has emerged as an independent and critical criterion
requiring systematic quantification and analysis 
1
. This section establishes the conceptual
framework for understanding fairness, defines starvation in the context of classical algorithms,
and outlines the specific challenges in measuring fairness across different scheduling paradigms.
1.1 Defining Fairness in Scheduling Contexts
Fairness in CPU scheduling can be conceptualized as the equitable distribution of processor
resources among competing processes. Unlike performance metrics that aim for optimization
(e.g., minimizing response time), fairness metrics seek to measure the equality or proportionality
of resource allocation. The challenge lies in its multidimensional nature—what constitutes "fair"
allocation depends on system objectives and process characteristics.
In the classic algorithms under review, fairness manifests in distinct ways:
• 
First-Come, First-Served (FCFS) provides fairness by treating all processes equally based on
2

. However, this

long ones suffer extended waiting times, revealing a limitation of this approach.
• 
Round Robin (RR) implements fairness through time-slicing, ensuring every process
receives an equal share of CPU time in a cyclic fashion. This prevents any single process from
monopolizing the CPU

6.3 Gaps in Current Fairness Evaluation Approaches
Our analysis of the literature reveals several limitations in prevailing methodologies:
1. 
2. 
Potential for Metric Misrepresentation: There is a noted risk that fairness metrics can
misrepresent the actual occurrence of job discrimination and starvation, which may lead to
incorrect conclusions about a scheduler's effectiveness 
11.
Over-reliance on Aggregate Measures: Many evaluations focus on aggregate metrics like
average response time but may fail to capture dynamic unfairness or the experience of
individual processes over short time scales.

Lack of Standardization: While sources like SPEC provide benchmarks for performance,
there is a lack of standardized workload traces and methodologies specifically annotated for
fairness evaluation across different research works.
6.4 Future Research Directions
To address these gaps and advance the field, we propose the following research directions:
1. 
2. 
3. 
4. 
5. 
Development of Context-Aware and Multi-Dimensional Metrics: Future work should
create fairness metrics that adapt to different application domains (real-time, interactive,
batch) and explicitly balance fairness with other objectives like efficiency, throughput, and
energy consumption. This involves moving beyond single-number fairness indices.
Enhanced Starvation Detection and Analysis: Research should focus on better methods for
detecting, quantifying, and visualizing starvation, especially in complex schedulers like
multilevel queues. This includes analyzing the conditions under which starvation occurs and
developing dynamic mitigation strategies.
Temporal and Dynamic Fairness Evaluation: New frameworks are needed to evaluate
fairness not just as a final aggregate, but as a property that evolves over time. This includes
studying short-term unfairness, recovery mechanisms, and fairness under transient, bursty
workloads.
Creation of Standardized Fairness Benchmarks: The community would benefit from an

fairness properties of schedulers, similar to how SPEC CPU stresses performance. This would
improve reproducibility and comparative analysis.
Fairness-Aware Algorithm Co-Design: Future scheduling algorithms should be designed
with formal fairness guarantees from the outset. Research should explore how to integrate
fairness constraints directly into scheduling logic while maintaining system efficiency and
exploring configurable fairness-efficiency trade-offs.
